About the North County Fire Protection District

What is the North County Fire Protection District?

The North County Fire Protection District (NCFPD) is an independent special district that provides fire protection, emergency medical services (EMS), and rescue services to the unincorporated communities of Fallbrook, Bonsall, and Rainbow in San Diego County. It is governed by an elected board of directors and funded primarily by local property taxes.

Why does the District exist?

Fallbrook, Bonsall, and Rainbow are unincorporated. That means there is no city government, no city council, and no city fire department. Special districts like NCFPD exist so unincorporated residents can still receive professional fire and emergency medical services — delivered locally, by people who answer directly to their neighbors.

This local control is a feature, not a bug. Decisions about staffing, equipment, and prevention get made by an elected board that lives where you live.

What areas does it serve?

NCFPD covers roughly 100 square miles of unincorporated North County San Diego, including:

  • Fallbrook
  • Bonsall
  • Rainbow
  • Surrounding rural and wildland-urban interface neighborhoods

Much of the District sits in some of California's highest wildfire-risk terrain.

What does the Fire Board do?

The board doesn't fight fires — but every decision firefighters operate under flows from board policy. Specifically, the board:

  • Sets the District's annual budget
  • Approves major contracts and capital purchases (apparatus, stations, tech)
  • Hires, evaluates, and supports the Fire Chief
  • Sets prevention and inspection policy
  • Represents residents in regional fire and emergency planning
  • Decides how transparent and accountable the District will be

Cole's Position

Does Cole support Zone 0 regulations?

No. Cole opposes strict Zone 0 mandates that require removing wooden fences, mulch, potted plants, and other common home features within 5 feet of structures.

He views them as unrealistic government overreach that burdens homeowners while ignoring the larger issue: fuel management on public lands. The wildfires that threaten our community don't start at a homeowner's mulch pile — they roll down from un-managed wildlands.

Instead, Cole strongly supports:

  • Practical Zone 1 (0–30 ft) and Zone 2 (30–100 ft) defensible space — what actually works
  • Voluntary home hardening with incentives, not mandates
  • Real wildland fuel management on public lands — thinning, grazing, prescribed burns
  • Education and resources for homeowners, not citations and fines

Voting & Election Information

When is the election?

The next California Statewide General Election is Tuesday, November 3, 2026. North County Fire Protection District Board seats appear on that ballot for eligible voters in the District.

How do I register to vote?

Register or update your registration online at the California Secretary of State: registertovote.ca.gov. You can register up to and including Election Day (same-day registration is available at any San Diego County voting location).

How do I vote by mail?

Every active registered voter in California is automatically mailed a ballot. You can return it by mail (postage paid), drop it at any official ballot drop box, or hand it in at a vote center. Track your ballot at wheresmyballot.sos.ca.gov.

Where do I vote in person?

San Diego County uses vote centers (not traditional precinct polling places). You can vote in person at any vote center in the county. Find locations and hours through the San Diego County Registrar of Voters at sdvote.com as the election approaches.

How does the Fire Board affect property taxes and home insurance?

The District is funded primarily by a portion of local property taxes plus benefit assessments. The board sets the budget and decides how those dollars are spent — staffing levels, equipment, prevention programs, station capital projects.

Insurance is downstream of all of that. A well-equipped, well-managed district with strong response times and aggressive fuel-reduction programs strengthens the community's case with insurers — and makes it harder for carriers to walk away from a region they've decided is too risky to write.

What is Cole's stance on fire codes and regulations?

Cole supports fire codes that are practical, evidence-based, and locally accountable. He supports modern building codes for new construction, defensible space requirements that actually reduce risk (Zones 1 and 2), and inspection programs that work with homeowners to fix problems.

What he opposes is regulation by press release — sweeping mandates handed down from Sacramento that burden residents without measurably improving safety. The Fire Board's job is to push back on those, not rubber-stamp them.
