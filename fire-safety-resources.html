Section 1

Wildfire Risk in Our Community

The Fallbrook–Bonsall–Rainbow region sits squarely in the wildland-urban interface (WUI) — where homes meet hills, canyons, and open chaparral. Sundowner and Santa Ana wind events, dry summers, and decades of fuel buildup on adjacent public lands combine to make this one of the highest wildfire-risk corridors in California.

The North County Fire Protection District (NCFPD) is our community's first line of defense in the unincorporated parts of that landscape. But the District can only do so much from a fire engine. The single biggest variable in whether a home survives an ember storm is the work the homeowner does before the fire arrives — defensible space and home hardening.

Section 2

Practical Defensible Space — Cole's Recommended Approach

California law and CAL FIRE guidance organize the area around your home into ember-resistance zones. The zones that consistently save homes — backed by post-fire research and decades of firefighter experience — are Zone 1 and Zone 2. That's where Cole wants the District's energy focused.

Zone 1 · 0–30 ft

Lean, Clean, and Green

  • Remove all dead plants, grass, and weeds
  • Clear dead leaves and pine needles from yard, roof, and gutters
  • Trim trees so branches stay at least 10 ft apart
  • Remove branches hanging over the roof or within 10 ft of a chimney
  • Relocate woodpiles to Zone 2
  • Remove or prune flammable shrubs near windows
  • Keep flammable items out from under decks, balconies, and stairs
Zone 2 · 30–100 ft

Reduced Fuel Zone

  • Cut or mow annual grass to a maximum height of 4 inches
  • Create horizontal spacing between shrubs and trees
  • Create vertical spacing between grass, shrubs, and tree canopies
  • Remove fallen leaves, needles, twigs, bark, cones, and small branches
  • Keep ladder fuels (low branches, brush under trees) cleared

Home Hardening — What Actually Stops an Ember

Most homes don't burn from a wall of flame. They burn because wind-blown embers find a vulnerability — a vent, a gutter full of needles, an unprotected eave. Hardening removes those weak points:

  • Class A roof (composition shingle, metal, tile)
  • Ember-resistant vents (1/8" or finer mesh, or rated assemblies)
  • Dual-pane windows with at least one tempered pane
  • Non-combustible siding in vulnerable areas (stucco, fiber cement, metal)
  • Enclosed (boxed) eaves
  • Gutter guards or non-combustible gutters kept clear of debris
  • Spark arrestors on chimneys
  • Non-combustible decking, or skirting that prevents ember intrusion underneath
Cole's Position on Zone 0

No to burdensome Zone 0 mandates.

Cole opposes strict Zone 0 mandates that require removing wooden fences, mulch, potted plants, and other common home features within five feet of a structure. He views them as unrealistic government overreach that burdens homeowners while ignoring the bigger fight: fuel management on public lands.

The fires that threaten our community don't start at a homeowner's mulch pile. They roll down from un-managed wildlands. Cole would rather see the District spend its credibility on:

  • Education and incentives — help homeowners harden, don't fine them
  • Aggressive fuel reduction on public lands — thinning, grazing, prescribed burns
  • Practical Zones 1 and 2 — the work that actually saves homes
  • Voluntary home hardening programs with real support, not paperwork

Emergency Preparedness

When a fire moves fast — and they all move fast in Santa Ana conditions — you do not have time to make a plan. You execute the plan you already have. Build it now.

Go Bag — 72-Hour Kit

  • Water — 1 gallon per person per day, 3 days minimum
  • Non-perishable food, manual can opener
  • Battery or hand-crank radio (NOAA weather capable)
  • Flashlight and spare batteries
  • First aid kit
  • N95 / dust masks (smoke)
  • Whistle, multi-tool or wrench/pliers
  • Phone chargers and a backup battery pack
  • Cash in small bills
  • Prescription medications (a week's supply)
  • Copies of ID, insurance, deed, medical info — in a waterproof pouch
  • Local maps; printed phone numbers (in case devices fail)
  • Change of clothes, sturdy shoes, work gloves
  • Pet supplies — leash, carrier, food, water, vaccination records

Evacuation Planning — “Ready, Set, Go!”

The CAL FIRE / IAFC Ready, Set, Go! framework is the gold standard. Internalize it now:

  1. READY — Be prepared. Defensible space done. Home hardened. Family plan written. Go bag packed.
  2. SET — Be alert. Pack the vehicle. Charge phones. Move livestock. Stage to leave.
  3. GO — Leave early, before evacuation orders if conditions warrant. Do not wait.

Family, Pet, and Livestock Plans

  • Family plan: agree on at least two evacuation routes, an out-of-area contact everyone calls, and a meeting point if you get separated.
  • Pets: carriers and leashes by the door, vaccination records in the go bag, a microchip on file. Identify pet-friendly evacuation lodging in advance.
  • Horses and livestock: trailer-train animals before fire season, pre-arrange large-animal evacuation through the San Diego Humane Society Emergency Response Team and equestrian neighbors, and post evacuation contacts at the barn.
  • Sign up for alerts: register for SD Emergency at alertsandiego.org and download the SD Emergency app.
